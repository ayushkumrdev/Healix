\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) grounds large language models (LLMs) in
domain knowledge, but two problems remain when the users are patients rather than
clinicians. Flat retrieval returns leaf passages that answer a specific question
well, yet a broad question such as ``how does chronic stress affect the body''
has no single passage that answers it; the answer is spread across many. And a
single dense embedding of a colloquial, under-specified query often fails to
match a corpus written in clinical register. We describe Healix, an on-device
medical companion, and a retrieval method we call \emph{altitude-matched
retrieval} that targets both problems together. A small language model drafts a
hypothetical answer at two levels of abstraction in one call; the specific
version joins the query to search the leaf passage index, and the broad version
searches a RAPTOR-style summary tree. Coupling Hypothetical Document Embeddings
(HyDE) with recursive summarization this way---matching each hypothetical to the
index tier of the same granularity---does not appear in prior work. Healix also
uses an agentic Mixture-of-Medical-Experts (MoME) router that gates queries to
specialist retrieval lenses at the system level, and it runs entirely offline on
one commodity GPU. We evaluate the stack with pre-registered hypotheses across four experiments:
paraphrased and keyword query variation, subtopic coverage on broad questions,
and end-to-end latency. The retrieval results are candidly negative---on this
curated corpus a strong dense encoder is near ceiling, hybrid fusion and HyDE add
no significant recall, and at a fixed evidence budget summary nodes cover
\emph{fewer} answer subtopics than leaf passages. The positive result is a
systems one: moving the query-side HyDE call to a sub-billion-parameter model
cuts end-to-end time-to-first-token from 14.7\,s to 6.4\,s ($2.3\times$), far
exceeding the isolated call's speedup, because on a single shared GPU the cost of
an auxiliary model is dominated by contention rather than its own size. We
discuss the safety posture of a deliberately disclaimer-free assistant and
release the system, benchmarks, and all artifacts as open source.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large language models can produce fluent medical explanations but hallucinate
facts they were not reliably trained on, and privacy concerns discourage sending
personal health questions to hosted APIs. Retrieval-augmented generation
(RAG)~\cite{lewis2020rag} mitigates the first problem by grounding generation in
retrieved evidence; running the entire pipeline locally mitigates the second.
Building such a system for lay medical use, however, exposes two failure modes
that standard RAG does not handle.

\textbf{The altitude gap.} Users ask questions at very different levels of
abstraction. ``What is the maximum daily dose of acetaminophen?'' is answered by
a single leaf passage. ``How does chronic stress affect the body overall?''
is not: no single passage contains the answer, which is an aggregate over
cardiovascular, immune, digestive, and neurological sources. Flat dense
retrieval, tuned to return the top passages, structurally cannot serve the
second class. Recursive summarization indexes such as
RAPTOR~\cite{sarthi2024raptor} add higher-level summary nodes to close this gap
on the \emph{index} side, but still search those nodes with the raw user query,
whose embedding sits far from any summary.

\textbf{The phrasing gap.} Patients phrase questions colloquially and
incompletely (``my chest feels tight when I'm stressed''), whereas the corpus is
written in clinical register. Single-vector dense retrieval blurs exact terms and
is sensitive to this mismatch. Hypothetical Document Embeddings
(HyDE)~\cite{gao2023hyde} address this on the \emph{query} side by embedding a
generated hypothetical answer instead of the question, but classic HyDE produces
one hypothetical against one flat index.

HyDE and RAPTOR act on the same axis---abstraction---from opposite ends, so they
can be combined. We generate a hypothetical at two altitudes in one
language-model call and send each to the tier whose granularity it matches: the
specific hypothetical joins the query for leaf retrieval, and the broad one
searches the summary tree. Because both come from a single call, the second
altitude costs nothing extra.

We integrate altitude-matched retrieval into Healix, a local-first medical
companion, alongside a system-level sparse Mixture-of-Medical-Experts router, a
hybrid dense/sparse retriever with reranking, and a persona-controlled generator,
all served offline from a single GPU. Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Altitude-matched retrieval}, a novel coupling of HyDE and RAPTOR in
which multi-granularity hypotheticals are routed to matching index tiers
(Section~\ref{sec:altitude}).
\item \textbf{Agentic Mixture-of-Medical-Experts (MoME)}, an embedding-gated
sparse router over specialist retrieval lenses that expands evidence coverage
without a second generation pass (Section~\ref{sec:moe}).
\item \textbf{An honest, pre-registered evaluation} of the full stack on
MedQuAD/PubMedQA---paraphrased and keyword query variation, broad-question
subtopic coverage, and end-to-end latency---whose retrieval results are largely
negative and reported as such, and whose positive finding is architectural: on a
shared single GPU, auxiliary-model cost is governed by contention, and offloading
the query-side model yields a $2.3\times$ end-to-end speedup
(Sections~\ref{sec:impl}--\ref{sec:eval}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Dense and hybrid retrieval.} Dense passage retrieval~\cite{karpukhin2020dpr}
maps queries and passages to a shared vector space; we use BGE
embeddings~\cite{xiao2024cpack} over a FAISS~\cite{johnson2019faiss} index.
Lexical BM25~\cite{robertson2009bm25} remains competitive for exact terms (drug
names, rare conditions); fusing the two with Reciprocal Rank
Fusion~\cite{cormack2009rrf} and cross-encoder
reranking~\cite{reimers2019sbert} is now standard practice, and Anthropic's
Contextual Retrieval~\cite{anthropic2024contextual} shows that prepending
situating context before indexing further improves recall. Healix uses all of
these as its base retriever.

\textbf{Query- and index-side augmentation.}
HyDE~\cite{gao2023hyde} embeds a generated hypothetical answer to bridge the
query--corpus register gap. RAPTOR~\cite{sarthi2024raptor} builds a tree of
recursive summaries so that broad queries can retrieve aggregated evidence. Both
are single-altitude: HyDE produces one hypothetical; RAPTOR searches its tree
with the raw query. We are not aware of prior work that generates hypotheticals
at multiple abstraction levels and matches each to a corresponding index tier.

\textbf{Mixture of experts and agents.} Sparsely-gated
MoE~\cite{shazeer2017moe} and Switch Transformers~\cite{fedus2022switch} route
tokens to expert sub-networks \emph{inside} a model. More recently,
mixture-of-agents approaches~\cite{wang2024moa} route at the system level across
LLM instances. Healix applies sparse gating one level up again---over medical
\emph{specialties}, each with its own retrieval lens---so that routing shapes
evidence gathering rather than parameter selection.

\textbf{Medical QA and safety.} MedQuAD~\cite{benabacha2019medquad} and
PubMedQA~\cite{jin2019pubmedqa} are widely used medical QA corpora and form our
knowledge base and evaluation source. Large medical LLMs such as
Med-PaLM~\cite{singhal2023medpalm} target clinician-grade benchmarks; Healix
instead targets lay users on-device, trading model scale for privacy,
controllability, and grounded retrieval.

\section{System Architecture}\label{sec:arch}
Healix processes each turn through the pipeline in Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}
(described textually here). An input message first passes a \emph{scope gate}
that resolves greetings, identity, and clearly non-medical requests to canned
responses with zero model cost, admitting only medical queries to the full
pipeline. For an admitted query the system (i) runs the MoME router to select
specialist lenses, (ii) generates two-altitude HyDE hypotheticals, (iii)
retrieves leaf evidence via hybrid dense/sparse search under each expert lens and
overview evidence from the RAPTOR tree, (iv) composes a single grounded prompt,
and (v) streams a persona-controlled answer from a local LLM. Persistent
conversation memory supplies continuity across turns. All components are served
offline; the LLM runs on a local Ollama server and every index is memory-mapped
from disk.

\section{Altitude-Matched Retrieval}\label{sec:altitude}
\subsection{Hierarchical index}
The leaf tier $L_0$ is the corpus of $N{=}239{,}644$ passage chunks embedded
with BGE and indexed in FAISS using 8-bit scalar quantization, chosen because it
preserves absolute cosine scores (mean score error $1.7\times10^{-4}$ versus a
flat index) so that score thresholds remain valid. We construct two summary
tiers offline. Leaf embeddings are reconstructed directly from the quantized
index (no re-embedding) and clustered with $k$-means into $K_1{=}1500$ topic
clusters; the local LLM summarizes each cluster's representative members into a
titled overview paragraph, forming tier $L_1$. The $L_1$ summaries are themselves
embedded and clustered into $K_2{=}120$ domain nodes ($L_2$). The build is
resumable and processes clusters largest-first so partial builds cover the
highest-mass topics. The resulting tree contains $1500{+}114{=}1614$ overview
nodes.

\subsection{Two-altitude hypotheticals}
Given query $q$, a single small-LM call emits two hypotheticals: a
\emph{specific} sentence $h_s$ carrying concrete clinical detail, and a
\emph{broad} sentence $h_b$ naming the overarching topic. We deliberately use a
sub-billion-parameter model here because the hypothetical is consumed only as an
embedding target, not shown to the user; a lenient parser falls back to the raw
generation when a small model ignores the output format, so the mechanism never
silently no-ops.

\subsection{Matched routing}
Leaf retrieval embeds the concatenation $[q ; h_s]$ and searches $L_0$ through
the full hybrid pipeline. Overview retrieval embeds $h_b$ (falling back to $q$)
and searches $L_1{\cup}L_2$; the top overview nodes are \emph{prepended} to the
evidence block so the generator reads the frame before the details. Formally, for
tier $L_t$ with granularity $g_t$, we search with the hypothetical $h$ whose
target granularity is closest to $g_t$:
\[
\text{evidence} \;=\; \bigcup_{t}\; \text{TopK}\big(L_t,\; \phi(h_{\arg\min_a |g_a-g_t|})\big),
\]
where $\phi$ is the shared embedding function. Classic HyDE is the special case
of a single tier and single hypothetical; classic RAPTOR is the special case of
searching all tiers with $\phi(q)$. Matching the hypothetical's altitude to the
tier aligns query and corpus geometry on both axes at once, and because both
hypotheticals come from one call, the second altitude is free. The mechanism is
\emph{fail-open}: any error, a missing tree, or an unavailable LM degrades
gracefully to standard hybrid retrieval on $q$.

\section{Agentic Mixture-of-Medical-Experts}\label{sec:moe}
Rather than route tokens (as in intra-model MoE) or whole LLM instances (as in
mixture-of-agents), Healix routes over medical \emph{specialties}. Each expert
$e$ carries (i) a semantic prototype $p_e$ encoded once, (ii) a keyword prior,
and (iii) a retrieval \emph{lens}---a query reformulation template. The gate
computes cosine similarity between the query embedding and each prototype, adds
keyword and imaging-modality boosts, and applies a sharpened softmax; the top-$k$
experts above a floor are activated:
\[
w_e = \mathrm{softmax}_\tau\!\big(\langle \phi(q), p_e\rangle + \beta\,\mathbb{1}[\text{kw}] + \gamma\,\mathbb{1}[\text{modality}]\big).
\]
Each activated expert reformulates $q$ through its lens and gathers
specialty-specific evidence; the union grounds a \emph{single} synthesis pass
framed as an integrated panel, so coverage improves without the cost of multiple
generations. The current roster covers Cardiology, Pulmonology, Neurology,
Dermatology, Gastroenterology, Musculoskeletal, Pharmacology, Psychophysiology,
and a default General expert. Activated experts and gate weights are surfaced to
the user as an explainability signal.

\section{Implementation and Latency Engineering}\label{sec:impl}
Healix is served by a FastAPI backend streaming tokens over Server-Sent Events to
a browser UI; generation uses a local Ollama server (default
\texttt{qwen2.5:7b-instruct}). The knowledge index (175\,MB quantized) and chunk
store are memory-mapped. Because the multi-stage pipeline runs several operations
\emph{before} the first generated token, query-side latency governs perceived
responsiveness, and HyDE is the largest such operation---it invokes a language
model on the critical path. Since the hypothetical is consumed only as an
embedding target, we run HyDE on a sub-billion-parameter model rather than the
generation model.

Two measurements quantify the effect (Table~\ref{tab:latency}), and their
difference is instructive. In isolation, the HyDE call itself shrinks only
modestly, from $3.76$\,s to $3.01$\,s (mean over 25 queries; paired bootstrap
95\% CI on the gap $[0.67, 0.85]$\,s). End to end on the warm server, however,
time-to-first-token falls from $14.7$\,s to $6.4$\,s and total latency from
$16.6$\,s to $8.7$\,s (mean over 10 queries each)---a $2.3\times$ reduction far
larger than the isolated saving. The gap is GPU contention: on a single 8\,GB
device the 7B HyDE call competes with the embedding model and cross-encoder
reranker for compute, so it runs much slower inside the live pipeline than alone.
Offloading it to a 0.5B model removes that contention. The lesson generalizes to
any multi-stage local pipeline that time-shares one accelerator: the cost of an
auxiliary model is dominated by contention, not by its own size. All stages remain
fail-open, so any component can be disabled or fail without breaking a response.

\section{Evaluation}\label{sec:eval}
We ran four experiments, each with its hypothesis stated before execution, and we
report every outcome including the failures. All harnesses, seeds, and result
files are released.

\subsection{Protocol}
Because the corpus questions appear verbatim in the index, evaluating retrieval
with the raw question is circular (dense retrieval trivially self-matches). We
therefore use a \emph{query-variation} protocol: we sample $n$ question--chunk
pairs, paraphrase each question into colloquial patient phrasing with an LLM,
keep the \emph{original chunk} as the gold target, and measure whether each
retrieval configuration recovers it in the top~$k$. This isolates robustness to
phrasing, exactly the property hybrid and HyDE claim to improve. We report
Recall@1/5/10 and MRR@10 for three configurations: \textbf{dense} (FAISS +
cross-encoder rerank), \textbf{hybrid} (dense + contextual BM25 + RRF + rerank),
and \textbf{hybrid+HyDE} (hybrid search over $[q;h_s]$). Sample size $n$, seed,
and paraphrase model are fixed and released with the code.

\subsection{Retrieval results}
Table~\ref{tab:retrieval} gives the benchmark, and the outcome is negative: the
three configurations differ by at most two points on any metric, share an
identical Recall@10 of $0.880$, and HyDE spends about three seconds per query
without improving recall. We report the full result rather than a favorable
slice. The parity has three likely causes. The cross-encoder reranker is strong
enough to equalize the candidate sets that dense and hybrid produce, which is the
expected behavior once reranking, not first-stage recall, decides the ordering.
An LLM paraphrase of an in-domain question also keeps most of its medical
content, so the dense encoder rarely misses by enough for lexical or hypothetical
augmentation to help. And the metric only credits recovery of one gold chunk, so
it cannot reward the aggregate-evidence case the hierarchical arm is built for.
The engineering consequence is clear: for in-domain single-target retrieval, the
cost of HyDE is not repaid in recall, which is why we keep it cheap and optional
rather than mandatory. Showing a win for hybrid or HyDE would need harder queries
than this benchmark contains---out-of-distribution phrasing or rare drug and gene
names---and building that set is the main evaluation gap we leave open.

\subsection{Keyword queries}
Dense encoders are trained on natural language and are expected to degrade on
terse keyword input, where BM25 should help. We repeated the protocol with
deterministic keyword queries (content words only, e.g. ``glaucoma treatment'')
over $n{=}500$ samples. The augmentations again did not help materially: hybrid
first-stage Recall@10 was $0.964$ versus $0.958$ for dense, a gap of $0.006$ with
a bootstrap 95\% CI of $[0.000, 0.014]$ that includes zero. Hybrid rescued three
queries that dense missed and lost none, so it never hurt, but the effect is not
significant. Reranking added a small, consistent precision gain (dense Recall@1
$0.916\rightarrow0.930$; hybrid $0.914\rightarrow0.936$). The BGE encoder handles
keyword queries well enough on this corpus that lexical fusion has little to
recover.

\subsection{Broad questions and the hierarchical arm}
We tested altitude matching directly on the regime it targets. We authored 24
broad questions, each with an objective set of textbook subtopics (e.g.,
complications of diabetes: retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy, cardiovascular,
foot, wound healing), and measured \emph{subtopic coverage} at a fixed 2000-character
evidence budget---counting how many subtopics appear in the retrieved text, which
controls for summary nodes being longer than leaf passages. The result is
negative and significant: flat leaf retrieval covered $0.67$ of subtopics on
average, the RAPTOR overview tier only $0.34$, and their union $0.33$ (overview
minus leaf gap $-0.33$, 95\% CI $[-0.46, -0.20]$). Inspection shows why: cluster
summaries generalize---``a range of complications''---and drop the specific terms
that leaf passages state outright, so on a corpus that already contains dedicated
passages per subtopic, summarization removes rather than adds coverage. Altitude
matching does not help here, and prepending overview nodes can displace more
informative leaf evidence. Where a summary tier would help is a corpus without
per-subtopic leaf coverage; establishing that is future work.

\subsection{System behavior}
Under a 5-way concurrent load the server completed all requests with isolated
conversation state and no errors, serializing generation behind a single lock.
Adversarial and out-of-domain inputs (prompt-injection attempts, non-medical
requests, empty and malformed messages) were refused or handled without leaking
system instructions.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\textbf{Safety posture.} Healix is deliberately disclaimer-free and avoids
ritual ``consult a professional'' closings, favoring specific, calm guidance.
This design choice trades a conventional safety net for readability and must be
revisited for any real deployment, particularly the handling of red-flag
emergency presentations, where suppressing urgent guidance would be harmful.
\textbf{Evaluation scope.} Our benchmark measures retrieval, not answer quality;
a human or LLM-judge study of grounded answer accuracy is the most important next
step. \textbf{Model ceiling.} The 7B generator bounds clinical nuance; the
architecture is model-agnostic and benefits directly from stronger local models.
\textbf{Paraphrase confound.} The query-variation protocol paraphrases questions
with an LLM. The retriever's encoder is a separate embedding model, so this is
less circular than an LLM-as-judge study, but paraphrase artifacts remain; a
human-paraphrased query set would be stronger. We fix the seed and release all
artifacts for reproduction.

\section{Conclusion}
We described Healix and altitude-matched retrieval, which couples HyDE and RAPTOR
by drafting hypotheticals at two granularities and routing each to the matching
tier of a hierarchical index, alongside a system-level Mixture-of-Medical-Experts
router. We then tested the stack the hard way, with pre-registered hypotheses,
and most of them failed: on a curated medical QA corpus a strong dense encoder
leaves essentially no recall headroom for hybrid fusion or HyDE, and summary
nodes cover fewer answer subtopics than the leaf passages they abstract. We take
these results seriously rather than explaining them away. They suggest a
practical rule for builders: measure whether the corpus already gives dense
retrieval per-subtopic leaf coverage before paying for augmentation machinery.
The durable positive finding is architectural---in a multi-stage pipeline
time-sharing one accelerator, auxiliary model cost is set by contention, not
parameter count, and offloading the query-side model yielded a $2.3\times$
end-to-end speedup. Whether altitude matching pays off on corpora \emph{without}
dense leaf coverage remains open, and our released benchmarks make that test
straightforward to run. Code and artifacts are public for reproduction.
